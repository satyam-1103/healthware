Deadlift

In its purest form, weightlifting involves lifting something and then lowering it again. This is, in a word, a deadlift. It is the epitome of simplicity and is one of the best exercises for muscle growth, strength and health.

How to do Deadlift


  1. 1. Do not place your feet shoulder-width apart or too far apart so that the socks are under the bar. The feet may be facing forward or slightly inclined. The heel should remain on the surface. When you wake up, the bar may move closer to your shin and hit your shin. The head (and eyes) should reflect the neutral position of the spine. While it doesn't visibly bend up or down, a slight upward tilt is unusual or unsafe with effort.
  2. 2. Stabilize the abdominal muscles by bracing them.
  3. 3. Squat down with your knees bent. The shape down to the bar is similar to (but not identical to) a squat, but the back is straight or slightly arched, and the shoulders and spine are not rounded.
  4. 4. Grasp the bar just outside the line of the knees with an overhand or mixed grip.
  5. 5. Lift the barbell by pushing it backwards from your knees. Exhale with effort. Be careful not to lift your hips first so your torso moves forward and your back rotates. Do not attempt to lift the bar by hand. Keep your arms outstretched in tension and grab the bar as your legs are pushed up. As a balance point, think of moving your legs and shoulders upward along with your hips.
  6. 6. The bar should almost touch your shins and stop at hip height when you reach maximum height. Pull your shoulders back as far as you can without bending your back.
  7. 7. Straighten your back again as you lower the bar to the floor in the opposite direction.
  8. 8. Repeat for your desired number of repetitions.

Pull Up

Pull-ups or Chin Ups are weight exercises that can cause severe biceps (and back) muscle growth with the pull-up bar alone. If your home gym has a pull-up bar attached to the door, that's all you need to do the pull-up. Because the lifter pulls its own weight, the biceps brachii is usually exposed to more stress than can be lifted with a barbell.

How to Do Pull Ups

  1. With your palms facing your hands, keep your hands shoulder-width apart or slightly wider and hang on the bar. From the dead slope, grab the shoulder blades together and pull your body up. Be careful not to fold your body inward until your chin is on or above the bar (many people do).

Inverted Row

The inverted deadlift is a bodyweight exercise that can develop back, arm, and grip strength like pull-ups. However, inverted deadlifts are usually easier to perform because they don't adjust your entire body weight. This is a great exercise for beginners to develop back strength and body control.

How to do Inverted Row

  1. 1. Stand in front of a squat rack or Smith machine.
  2. 2. Set the bar to the favored setting. Start with waist height. This will permit your hands to absolutely enlarge whilst retaining your frame off the floor.
  3. 3. Get under the bar and lie down. Look up at the ceiling.
  4. 4. Reach up for the bar. Your arms should be fully extended so you can grab the bar with an overhand grip. Your body will be suspended or just off the floor, with your heels being the only thing in contact with the floor.
  5. 5. Tighten your core and glutes to strengthen your lower back and keep your body in a straight line from your torso to your feet.
  6. 6. Pull up to your chest. The bar or ring should be at chest level at the top of the movement. Your body should keep your glutes and core in place throughout the movement. The bar should not touch your chest. Bring it as close as possible.
  7. 7. Pause, make sure your shoulder blades are pulled together (imagine holding a small ball between your shoulder blades), then slowly lower back to the starting position with your arms fully extended.
  8. 8. Repeat. Do 3 sets of 8 to 15 reps.
